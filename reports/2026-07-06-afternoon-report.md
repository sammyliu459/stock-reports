## Federal Reserve Analysis

The Federal Reserve's monetary policy stance remains the central focus for market participants as we progress through the summer of 2026. Following the June FOMC meeting, the Federal Reserve maintained interest rates in the range of 3.50% to 3.75%, but signaled a more accommodative stance with the release of the dot plot projections suggesting two potential rate cuts before year-end.

The Fed's current policy trajectory reflects a delicate balancing act between supporting economic growth and maintaining price stability. Market participants are now pricing in approximately 3-4 additional 25-basis-point rate cuts by the end of 2026, which would bring the federal funds rate to a range of 2.50% to 2.75%. This more dovish outlook has provided significant support for risk assets, particularly growth stocks and technology companies with longer-duration cash flows.

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell has emphasized the data-dependent nature of future policy decisions, indicating that the pace and timing of rate cuts will be heavily influenced by incoming inflation data, labor market conditions, and broader economic indicators. The Fed's preferred inflation measure, the Core PCE Price Index, has shown gradual progress toward the 2% target, though sticky components such as shelter and services inflation continue to pose challenges.

The central bank's balance sheet normalization program continues in the background, with the Fed allowing Treasury and mortgage-backed securities to roll off at a measured pace of approximately $25 billion per month. This quantitative tightening, combined with the still-elevated level of interest rates relative to historical norms, has contributed to tighter financial conditions that are gradually working their way through the economy.

The Fed's Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) released at the June meeting revealed modest adjustments to the economic outlook. The median projection for GDP growth in 2026 was revised slightly higher to 2.0%, while the unemployment rate forecast was nudged up to 4.2%. The inflation outlook showed gradual improvement, with Core PCE inflation expected to reach 2.3% by year-end.

For investors, the Fed's dovish pivot represents a significant tailwind for risk assets. Lower interest rates reduce the discount rate applied to future cash flows, supporting higher valuations for growth stocks. However, the pace of rate cuts may be constrained if inflation proves more persistent than currently anticipated or if the labor market remains stronger than expected, creating potential volatility in rate-sensitive sectors.

The fixed income market has responded to the Fed's dovish signals with a steepening of the yield curve, as longer-term rates have declined more than short-term rates. This dynamic has provided some relief to banks and other financial institutions that had been struggling with the inverted yield curve environment.

## Economic Data Analysis

The U.S. economy continues to demonstrate remarkable resilience as we enter the second half of 2026, maintaining steady growth despite the restrictive monetary policy environment of the past two years. Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth has held steady at an annualized rate of approximately 2.1% in recent quarters, above the economy's long-term potential and suggesting continued underlying strength.

The labor market remains robust, with the unemployment rate holding steady near 4.1%, a level consistent with full employment by most measures. Nonfarm payrolls have continued to expand at a healthy pace, with the economy adding an average of 185,000 jobs per month over the past quarter. While this pace has moderated from the torrid job creation of 2021-2022, it remains sufficient to absorb new entrants to the labor force and maintain wage growth.

Wage growth has shown signs of gradual cooling, with average hourly earnings increasing at a year-over-year rate of approximately 3.6%, down from peaks above 5% in 2022 but still elevated by historical standards. The moderation in wage growth is encouraging from an inflation perspective, as it reduces the risk of a wage-price spiral while still providing real income gains to workers as inflation declines.

Inflation data has been generally encouraging, with the Consumer Price Index (CPI) moderating to approximately 2.8% year-over-year, down significantly from the 9.1% peak reached in June 2022. Core inflation, which excludes volatile food and energy prices, has been more persistent, running at approximately 3.3% year-over-year. The Fed's preferred measure, the Core PCE Price Index, has shown similar improvement but remains at 2.7%, above the 2% target.

Housing market activity has shown signs of stabilization following the sharp correction of 2022-2023. Existing home sales have improved modestly as mortgage rates have declined from their peaks, and new home construction has picked up as builders respond to pent-up demand. The S&P CoreLogic Case-Shiller Home Price Index has resumed its upward trajectory, with home prices rising approximately 5.2% year-over-year.

The manufacturing sector has shown encouraging signs of recovery, with the ISM Manufacturing PMI returning to expansion territory at 50.9 in the most recent reading. New orders and production indices have improved, suggesting that the manufacturing recession that plagued much of 2024 may be coming to an end. The services sector remains robust, with the ISM Services PMI holding above 53, indicating continued expansion.
